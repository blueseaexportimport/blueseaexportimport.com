  <summary>Banned Synthetic Food Additives</summary>
  
<p style="font-size:14px !important;border:0;" >


<br>
<span>In our opinion, all synthetic food additives are bad.</span><br> 
<span>Food additives are chemicals you eat, same as you swallow medicines.</span><br> 
<span style="color:darkred;" ><b>If you request products that must contain synthetic food additives, we will reject to collaborate with you.</b></span><br><br> 
<span>Regulation (EC) No 1333/2008 of the European Parliament and of the Council of 16 December 2008 on food additives</span>
<br><br>
<span><b>Article 3 - Definitions</b></span>
<br>
<span><b>(a) ‘food additive’ shall mean any substance not normally consumed as a food in itself and not normally used as a characteristic ingredient of food, whether or not it has nutritive value, the intentional addition of which to food for a technological purpose in the manufacture, processing, preparation, treatment, packaging, transport or storage of such food results, or may be reasonably expected to result, in it or its by-products becoming directly or indirectly a component of such foods;</b></span><br><br>

<span><b>The following are not considered to be food additives:</b></span><br>
<span>(i) monosaccharides, disaccharides or oligosaccharides and foods containing these substances used for their sweetening properties;</span><br>
<span>(ii) foods, whether dried or in concentrated form, including flavourings incorporated during the manufacturing of compound foods, because of their aromatic, sapid or nutritive properties together with a secondary colouring effect;</span><br>
<span>(iii) substances used in covering or coating materials, which do not form part of foods and are not intended to be consumed together with those foods;</span><br>
<span>(iv) products containing pectin and derived from dried apple pomace or peel of citrus fruits or quinces, or from a mixture of them, by the action of dilute acid followed by partial neutralisation with sodium or potassium salts (liquid pectin);</span><br>
<span>(v) chewing gum bases;</span><br>
<span>(vi) white or yellow dextrin, roasted or dextrinated starch, starch modified by acid or alkali treatment, bleached starch, physically modified starch and starch treated by amylolitic enzymes;</span><br>
<span>(vii) ammonium chloride;</span><br>
<span>(viii) blood plasma, edible gelatin, protein hydrolysates and their salts, milk protein and gluten;</span><br>
<span>(ix) amino acids and their salts other than glutamic acid, glycine, cysteine and cystine and their salts having no technological function;</span><br>
<span>(x) caseinates and casein;</span><br>
<span>(xi) inulin;</span><br>
<br><br>


<span><b>(b)  ‘processing aid’ shall mean any substance which:</b></span><br><br>

<span>(i) is not consumed as a food by itself;</span>
<span>(ii) is intentionally used in the processing of raw materials, foods or their ingredients, to fulfil a certain technological purpose during treatment or processing; and</span><br>
<span>(iii) may result in the unintentional but technically unavoidable presence in the final product of residues of the substance or its derivatives provided they do not present any health risk and do not have any technological effect on the final product;</span>
<br><br>

<span><b>(c) ‘functional class’ shall mean one of the categories set out in Annex I based on the technological function a food additive exerts in the foodstuff;</b></span>
<br><br>

<span><b>(d) ‘unprocessed food’ shall mean a food which has not undergone any treatment resulting in a substantial change in the original state of the food, for which purpose the following in particular are not regarded as resulting in substantial change: dividing, parting, severing, boning, mincing, skinning, paring, peeling, grinding, cutting, cleaning, trimming, deep-freezing, freezing, chilling, milling, husking, packing or unpacking;</b></span>
<br><br>

<span><b>(e) ‘food with no added sugars’ shall mean a food without the following:</b></span><br>
<span>(i) any added monosaccharides or disaccharides;</span><br>
<span>(ii)  any added food containing monosaccharides or disaccharides which is used for its sweetening properties;</span>
<br><br>

<span><b>(f)  ‘energy-reduced food’ shall mean a food with an energy value reduced by at least 30 % compared with the original food or a similar product;</b></span>
<br><br>

<span><b>(g)  ‘table-top sweeteners’ shall mean preparations of permitted sweeteners, which may contain other food additives and/or food ingredients and which are intended for sale to the final consumer as a substitute for sugars;</b></span>
<br><br>

<span><b>(h) ‘quantum satis’ shall mean that no maximum numerical level is specified and substances shall be used in accordance with good manufacturing practice, at a level not higher than is necessary to achieve the intended purpose and provided the consumer is not misled.</b></span>
<br><br>

<span><b>ANNEX I - Functional classes of food additives in foods and of food additives in food additives and food enzymes</b></span><br><br>
<span><b>sweeteners</b> are substances used to impart a sweet taste to foods or in table-top sweeteners;</span><br>
<span><b>colours</b> are substances which add or restore colour in a food, and include natural constituents of foods and natural sources which are normally not consumed as foods as such and not normally used as characteristic ingredients of food. Preparations obtained from foods and other edible natural source materials obtained by physical and/or chemical extraction resulting in a selective extraction of the pigments relative to the nutritive or aromatic constituents are colours within the meaning of this Regulation;</span><br>
<span><b>preservatives</b> are substances which prolong the shelf-life of foods by protecting them against deterioration caused by micro-organisms and/or which protect against growth of pathogenic micro-organisms;</span><br>
<span><b>antioxidants</b> are substances which prolong the shelf-life of foods by protecting them against deterioration caused by oxidation, such as fat rancidity and colour changes;</span><br>
<span><b>carriers</b> are substances used to dissolve, dilute, disperse or otherwise physically modify a food additive or a flavouring, food enzyme, nutrient and/or other substance added for nutritional or physiological purposes to a food without altering its function (and without exerting any technological effect themselves) in order to facilitate its handling, application or use;</span><br>
<span><b>acids</b> are substances which increase the acidity of a foodstuff and/or impart a sour taste to it;</span><br>
<span><b>acidity regulators</b> are substances which alter or control the acidity or alkalinity of a foodstuff;</span><br>
<span><b>anti-caking agents</b> are substances which reduce the tendency of individual particles of a foodstuff to adhere to one another;</span><br>
<span><b>anti-foaming agents</b> are substances which prevent or reduce foaming;</span><br>
<span><b>bulking agents</b> are substances which contribute to the volume of a foodstuff without contributing significantly to its available energy value;</span><br>
<span><b>emulsifiers</b> are substances which make it possible to form or maintain a homogenous mixture of two or more immiscible phases such as oil and water in a foodstuff;</span><br>
<span><b>emulsifying salts</b> are substances which convert proteins contained in cheese into a dispersed form and thereby bring about homogenous distribution of fat and other components;</span><br>
<span><b>firming agents</b> are substances which make or keep tissues of fruit or vegetables firm or crisp, or interact with gelling agents to produce or strengthen a gel;</span><br>
<span><b>flavour enhancers</b> are substances which enhance the existing taste and/or odour of a foodstuff;</span><br>
<span><b>foaming agents</b> are substances which make it possible to form a homogenous dispersion of a gaseous phase in a liquid or solid foodstuff;</span><br>
<span><b>gelling agents</b> are substances which give a foodstuff texture through formation of a gel;</span><br>
<span><b>glazing agents</b> (including lubricants) are substances which, when applied to the external surface of a foodstuff, impart a shiny appearance or provide a protective coating;</span><br>
<span><b>humectants</b> are substances which prevent foods from drying out by counteracting the effect of an atmosphere having a low degree of humidity, or promote the dissolution of a powder in an aqueous medium;</span><br>
<span><b>modified starches</b> are substances obtained by one or more chemical treatments of edible starches, which may have undergone a physical or enzymatic treatment, and may be acid or alkali thinned or bleached;</span><br>
<span><b>packaging gases</b> are gases other than air, introduced into a container before, during or after the placing of a foodstuff in that container;</span><br> 
<span><b>propellants</b> are gases other than air which expel a foodstuff from a container;</span><br>
<span><b>raising agents</b> are substances or combinations of substances which liberate gas and thereby increase the volume of a dough or a batter;</span><br>
<span><b>sequestrants</b> are substances which form chemical complexes with metallic ions;</span><br>
<span><b>stabilisers</b> are substances which make it possible to maintain the physico-chemical state of a foodstuff; stabilisers include substances which enable the maintenance of a homogenous dispersion of two or more immiscible substances in a foodstuff, substances which stabilise, retain or intensify colour of a foodstuff and substances which increase the binding capacity of the food, including the formation of cross-links between proteins enabling the binding of food pieces into re-constituted food;</span><br>
<span><b>thickeners</b> are substances which increase the viscosity of a foodstuff;</span><br>
<span><b>flour treatment agents</b> are substances, other than emulsifiers, which are added to flour or dough to improve its baking quality;</span><br>
<span><b>contrast enhancers</b> are substances which, when applied to the external surface of fruit or vegetables following depigmentation of predefined parts (e.g. by laser treatment), help to distinguish these parts from the remaining surface by imparting colour following interaction with certain components of the epidermis."</span><br>



</p>
